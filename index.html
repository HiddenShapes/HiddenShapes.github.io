1 Introductions

motivation

The fact we’re kicking this off with Pappus’ theorem is a big surprise (to me at least). You see, I’ve never been one to say things like all of maths is beautiful. Don’t get me wrong, I love maths. Most of my friends would agree, I get too excited about some maths. But Pappus’ theorem… well, let’s just take a look at it.

Pappus’ theorem.

Let \(\{A,B,C\}\) and \(\{X,Y,Z\}\) be two distinct triples of collinear points in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) such that the lines \((A,B)\) and \((X,Y)\) don’t coincide. Then the points \((A,Y) \cap (B,X)\), \((A,Z) \cap (C,X)\) and \((B,Z) \cap (C,Y)\) are also collinear.

Before proceeding, we’d better make sure we’re all on the same page, at least in terms of terminology. Three points are called collinear if they all lie on a common line, \(\mathbb{R}^2\) is the set of pairs of real numbers and the \(\cap\) symbol denotes intersection. From now on when technical terms come up, I’ll give their definition in a footnote like this one.1 While we’re discussing the conventions of this document, any brightly coloured dots in a figure should be interactive, try moving them along the lines in the Pappus configuration above. You can also pan and zoom to get a better view if stuff moves off-screen.

Hopefully you can see what I was driving at before: Pappus’ theorem is just a bit dry. It falls right inline with Pythagoras and Thales’ theorems as the kind of thing mathematicians are always trying to convince people don’t form the entirety of the subject. Well, our goal today is to uncover a truly interesting bit of structure underlying this seemingly innocuous result. The journey there will take us through a variety of mathematical disciplines: projective geometry, topology, computer assisted proofs…

However, before any of that, we’re going to have a look at a proof of Pappus. And I warn you, the proof of Pappus’ theorem is even more boring that the statement. But that’s kind of the point. You’ll see.
